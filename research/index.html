Dissertation

Digital Divides and Dialogues: A Multidimensional Analysis of Populist Rhetoric, Social Media Interaction, and Societal Polarization in Latin America

Abstract: What are the effects of Latin American populist presidents' use of social media platforms? This question has attracted some scholarly attention in recent years. However, the nuances of populist rhetoric disseminated by these actors on social media, the public's reactions to such messages, and the broader implications for the digital ecosystem—particularly in developing democracies—remain largely unexplored. Existing scholarship on populist actors has primarily focused on candidates, party leaders, or heads of state. I argue that populists in office differ fundamentally from populist candidates or party leaders. Once elected, populists tend to adopt a twofold strategy to advance their agenda: they divide society into two opposing camps and seek to consolidate a popular majority that will support them in electoral contests. Given the unmediated communication channels that social media offer, these platforms are intensively used by populists in power to reinforce this strategy. As a result, their supporters adopt plebiscitary attitudes, expressing unwavering support, while opponents respond with heightened polarization. These reciprocal dynamics reinforce one another, ultimately serving the populist's broader strategic goals.
